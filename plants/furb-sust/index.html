Furb-Sust

Dec 22, 2018 3 min read

In August 2018 I embarked on a project focused on developing skills for refurbishing items and improving my personal sustainability practices to reduce my and my community’s wastefulness. I designed this activity around ideas in Jane Mcgonigal’s Superbetter. The premise of Superbetter is that a game-ful mindset helps people find meaning, achieve their goals, be happier, and be better contributors to their community.

Using Mcgonigal’s framework I identified my epic win as:

Epic Win: Learn and demonstrate eight new furb-sust (refurbishing or sustainability) practices (roughly one a week).

I then made a list of potential quests that would help me achieve this Epic Win. The initial list included:

  • Fix a hole in a piece of clothing
  • Fix a piece of a furniture
  • Set up a composting system
  • Measure, record, and analyze the amount of trash that I produce
  • Refurbish old electronics into something useful
  • Instead of throwing something away, find a new use for it
  • Fix a bad zipper

Over time I added more quests to fulfill the Epic Win.

Quests Completed

As of this writing, way more than eight weeks later, I have completed six and a half quests.

  1. Fix a hole in a piece of clothing (hole ripped open again, need to fix)
  2. Setup a compost system (took three tries): Got a bin and drive it to a compost drop every few months.
  3. Set up system for drying clothes: I made an indoor clothes hanging line. It fell last weekend.
  4. Set up sustainable system for managing kitty waste: I bought Litter Genie.
  5. Grow edible plants in the winter: Grew salad greens indoors.
  6. Re-work old candles into new candles: Did just this and have made more candles from beeswax since.
  7. Join a food co-op: Joined a fermentation co-op.
  8. Sign up for CSA: Signed up for a vegetable half-share for summer 2019.

Lessons Learned

  • Making a list of tasks and letting me pick which one I want to complete when is a really really good system for me. It reminds me what I want to do over a period of time, in which I can easily forget what I want to do. It helps me track what I’ve done because I can look at the completed quests. It gets me excited to do research, get inspired, and find ideas.
  • Completing a task, developing a system, and developing a system that requires little input are all different things. In the set up a compost system task, I opted to not subscribe to a pick-up compost service because my two options were expensive (it’s not provided by my municipality). So I instead bough compost bags and intended to take a full bag to the compost drop when I play soccer once a week. However, when I forgot to take the bag the first week, the compost ate through the bag and it was useless. I then didn’t want to take the compost in my care in fear that it would get all over everything. So I started to compile tons of compost without a plan of getting rid of it. I now need to carefully drive to the compost drive every other month to dump the compost — this is a considerable regular task since it takes about 35 minutes total, I usually don’t couple the activity with any other errands, and it causes me some anxiety. In whole, this is not a great system. I want to re-think this, but this version achieves my task and is do-able for now.
  • Overall, one can complete a task, create a system that requires high effort, or create a system with low to no effort. I aim for the latter but have found it exceedingly difficult.
